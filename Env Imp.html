Environmental Impacts of Land Meat Production & Consumption

Meat is a common part of Americans’ diet as well as many people globally. What some may or may not be aware of are the environmental impacts of meat consumption, particularly land meat consumption. (Land meat refers to beef, pork, and poultry, as opposed to the meat of fish.) The world’s population is over 7 billion right now with projections to reach 9 billion by 2050. As we endure a steadily growing population, our consumption stresses the food production systems and shifts it in certain directions. So while the consumption of land meat itself isn’t a particular driver of climate change, land meat consumption on the aggregate contributes to the support of these poor production practices. Ultimately, the ability to produce land meat cannot keep up with global demand without taking shortcuts, namely those that are against the environment.

Land meat production on a fundamental level is unsustainable. (“Unsustainable” is meant to describe the availability of a resource or good for future generations, while “green” refers to practices that are aware of their environmental impacts on all levels.) On the basis of trophic levels land meat production is inefficient. There is an inefficient energy transfer when say 100 units of energy are generated by a primary producer, once it is consumed by a non-human herbivore, it only retains 1/10 of that energy (10 units). So if a human eats the herbivore or carnivore (the next trophic level up) versus the primary producer, it is only gaining a fraction of the potential energy available. Due to such inefficiencies, one pound of meat takes 440 gallons of water and one pound of quality steak takes over 2000 gallons of water. Additionally, with the amount of grain fed to livestock has the potential to feed 800 million people.

Land meat production is not only inefficient and unsustainable but it is a contributing factor towards climate change. It is estimated that 18% of global GHG emissions come from meat production and consumption practices. With the growing population has come growing meat consumption and therefore need for livestock and land devoted towards livestock. Deforestation has become a problem and land is converted for meat operations because trees act as sinks that store carbon tens of thousands of years. When they’re cut down that releases trapped carbon dioxide into the atmosphere and also moves the capacity for earth’s tree stock to sequester carbon. Secondly, pollution from runoff of these meat operations is contaminated with artificial fertilizers, pesticides, and other antibiotics. This leads to dead aquatic zones where there is too much contamination for biotic life. Finally, meat production requires the input of energy in the form of fossil fuels which are direct causes of GHG emissions.

Environmental Impacts of Unsustainable Aquaculture


As a method to help combat the environmental impacts of growing land meat consumption, it is thought that shifting to more fish consumption would help reduce ecological degradation, and that farming fish (aquaculture) would be a solution to supplying protein for a rapidly growing population with stressed resourced. While fish can multiply at a faster rate among other reasons making them candidates to be compactly and quickly farmed, not all aquaculture operations are considerate of the environmental implications caused as a result. Some aquaculture are unsustainable in the rate at which they extract fish from the ocean. This can lead to a sustainable fish farm but leaves the wild stocks struggling to maintain. Many aquaculture productions are not sustainable or green in the type of feed they use. The feed is made up “trash fish” or otherwise low valued fish. Most times the fish being farmed are not carnivorous and even if fed the pellets they’re fed contain essential nutrients, it should not have components of other fish.

Organic wastes cause toxic effluents to enter the ocean resulting with dead zones and algae bloom. Excess feed, fish waste, and dead fish contaminate the nearby water source proving by using common aquaculture practices, the sustainable goal of having enough fish may be met, but the green intentions are completely overlooked. Moreover, these poor practicing aquaculture operations degrade local coastal ecosystems which influences the biotic community, the potential economic viability, and the surrounding aesthetics.
