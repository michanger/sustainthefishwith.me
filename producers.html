--GLOSSARY OF MAJOR AQUACULTURE FARMS IN CALIFORNIA--

Oysters

          The Oldest and most visible aquaculture industry in California. It dates as far back as the 1850â€™s and to this day California is the largest oyster-producing state. The industry collapsed with pollution in the San Francisco Bay in 1939. It was revitalized in the 1950â€™s with Japanese seed oysters. Today, there is a thriving network of west coast fisheries exchanging oyster seed and harvesting them from the Humboldt, Tomales, Morro, and San Diego Bays, as well as offshore sites in Santa Barbra. The common methods of acquiring lands is to lease them from the state, municipalities, or public utilities. Other mollusks grown or under research include shellfish, mussels, abalone, and clams.

Channel Catfish

          The most profitable aquaculture industry in the state. Most production occurs in the Great Central, Imperial, and Coachella valleys of Southern California. The fish require warm waters and production in Northern California has been expanding at geothermal sites. The farms have from 2-400 acres of surface water, and produce 1,500 to 12,000 lbs per acre. The catfish market in California is unique with minimal post-processing. Most fish is sold live to feed the billion dollar recreational fishing industry, and live tank seafood markets. Much of the product is targeted towards the Asian and Southeast Asian communities in large cities. Other large buyers are restaurants, national fish processers, and retail.

Salmonoids and Trout

          Rainbow trout and other salmonids make up the next most important aquaculture industry in California. Production is located near artesian springs and lower discharge water in reservoirs. These types of fishing require moving water- and output is measured at 15,000 lbs per 450 gallons a minute per year of water flow. Again, much of the trout are marketed to recreational fee-fishing lakes. Similar to catfish, there exist little processing facilities. Bid contracts for recreational lakes usually cost 1.45 to 1.75 per pound, live weight.
          Trout eggs are also sold nationally and internationally. R&D Efforts have created a year-round high-quality egg that makes California competitive. About 80-100 million eggs are moved annually. Farmed salmon are raised on land in Northern California, then brought down to coastal tanks to be transitioned to salt water. They then are sold to market, or used in an innovative program with northwest fisheries where small fish from California are exchanged with large fishes to augment each regionâ€™s processes.

Baitfish, ornamental fish, crustaceans, and live feed

          Not for human consumption, but noted due to the surprisingly large size and scope of this sector. This supports aquariums, or feed for other farmed fish. The hyper-saline ponds in the San Francisco Bay Area and the desert parts of Southern California are used to raise larval shrimp, again for feed. California aquaculture even provides worms for use in water treatment ponds and aquarium feed. They are interesting in that they are raised on the back end of trout systems, thus promoting a sustainable synergy.
